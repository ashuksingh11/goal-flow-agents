Good call keeping this at the design level. Before I list failure modes, one reframe that changes _which_ failures even matter:

**The highest-leverage unknown is who the audience is and what single thing they must walk away believing.** Leadership greenlight, technical review, and partner pitch pull the demo in different directions — for leadership the star is the experience plus a "we've thought about the architecture" reassurance; for a technical review the star is the two-tier boundary and the harnesses themselves. We haven't pinned this, and it decides what to foreground and what to cut. I'll flag below where it matters.

**Problems I'd worry about, roughly in priority order:**

- **The differentiation is invisible — this is the one that would actually sink it.** If the demo just shows "I asked the fridge, it gave a meal plan," the audience sees a chatbot. The two-tier orchestration, the harnesses, the safety gate, the human-in-the-loop are all machinery they never see. A demo that runs flawlessly can still fail this way. The design has to _deliberately surface_ the orchestration, or the IP stays hidden.
- **Live LLM nondeterminism and latency in the hot path.** You have two LLM calls on the critical path (cloud understanding, device planning), one of them on-device over the Hub's network. Live, that's 10–30s of possible silence and a different answer every run — one run the planner slips in a disliked ingredient or phrases the plan oddly, and it reads as flaky in front of leadership. Rehearsed demos hate nondeterminism.
- **The adaptation beat is your most fragile moment.** The football-match re-plan is the most compelling proof that it isn't a one-shot chatbot, but it's also the most complex: virtual clock, watcher, materiality, event timing, the datetime discipline. It's the single thing most likely to desync live.
- **The UI is under-discussed, and it's what they look at.** We've designed the brains and said almost nothing about the surface on the Hub screen. A janky UI undercuts an otherwise polished demo, and a Tizen webview UI is real work — under-invest and it hurts, over-invest and you've spent your time on the wrong thing.
- **On-device fragility** — lower now, given Linux-first plus the fallback, but make sure the laptop fallback is genuinely demo-ready rather than theoretical, and rehearse the WebSocket-drop reconnect.

**Ways to simplify — what I'd actually do:**

- **Pin the LLM to the scripted scenario.** Fix the profile, the goal, and the world so both LLM calls produce the same known output every run — low temperature, pinned/cached responses, a canned fallback on timeout. You can go further and make the _device_ planner deterministic (rules over inventory) for the demo: the harnesses still all run, the device is fast and reproducible, and the one visible "intelligence" moves to the cloud where you control it. Trade-off: you lose "the device itself is smart," but keep everything the pattern proves.
- **Fix the world and make it resettable.** Seed inventory and calendar so the plan and the "missing items" proposal are predictable, and give yourself a one-button reset so you can re-run cleanly (you'll often demo twice). A rehearsed demo wants a fixed world, not a random one.
- **Two acts, only one fully live.** Act 1 = goal → plan → approve → execute, live and tight. Act 2 = the week-long adaptation — but instead of a fully emergent virtual-clock event, _narrate it from the trace log_ ("here's what it did Wednesday when the match appeared") or lightly script it. You keep the "long-running, adaptive" headline while removing the most fragile live machinery.
- **Cut or script the clarification round.** It's a nice "understands fuzzy goals" beat, but it's an extra LLM call and round-trip and it isn't what proves the two-tier orchestration. Show it once, scripted, or drop it.
- **Turn the machinery visible — and make your operator panel do double duty.** The best single fix for the invisibility problem: put a live "behind the scenes" view on a second screen showing the message flow and the harnesses lighting up — contract down, pre-check ✓, capability ✓, grounding, planner, gate: passed, proposal up. That trace _is_ the IP made visible. And it's the same control panel you already need for the clock and events, so it's dual-use, not extra work.

Net, and this is where I'd push back on the current spec: the demo as drawn — full loop, two live LLMs, on-device, live adaptation — is more than a reliable first demo needs. I'd make Act 1 rock-solid and reproducible, narrate Act 2 from the trace, and spend the freed-up effort making the orchestration visible. A chatbot that works is less impressive than an architecture the audience can watch think.
